Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows verification time against
circuit depth for a chain of $\ell$ cascaded ISW-AND gadgets, each computing
$y^{(k)} = y^{(k-1)} \odot x^{(k)}$ with fresh randomness and each secure at every
length.
The time axis is logarithmic, so the near-straight lines show verification cost
growing exponentially in the chain length even though the netlist grows only
linearly ($8$ wires per stage). The cause is that the canonical DAG of a deep
wire grows with its multiplicative depth. The coupling engine (solid lines) runs a
constant factor faster than the naive, no-extension baseline (dashed lines) shares
its asymptotics; this is the scalability boundary of the present representation.
Measured on an AMD Ryzen~7 5800H.

  Table~\ref{tab:extension-comparison} compares the three extension mechanisms of
  Chapter~\ref{chap:design}, coupling-driven (C, Section~\ref{sec:coupling-extension}),
  witness replay (R, Section~\ref{sec:replay}), and closure-driven (Cl,
  Section~\ref{sec:closures}), across the benchmark suite. Each entry is a C/R/Cl
  triple giving the verdict (S~$=$~\Secure{}, I~$=$~\Insecure{}), the wall-clock
  time, the number of rewrite-engine discharges, and the extension yield (\emph{free
  wires}, the wires certified beyond the chosen probes). Replay is factoring-free by
  design, mirroring \texttt{maskVerif}, so it false-reports \Insecure{} on the five
  factoring-dependent rows marked $\dagger$, where the coupling and closure engines,
  which keep factoring, agree. The discharge and free-wire counts are the
  mechanism-fair metrics; wall-clock is indicative only, since the closure engine
  predates several later optimisations, and a controlled timing comparison is given
  in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling}. All runs are on an AMD Ryzen~7 5800H.
